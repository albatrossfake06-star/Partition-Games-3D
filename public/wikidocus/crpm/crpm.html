CRPM

Partizan Integer Partition Combinatorial Game

Let $[r_1, r_2, r_3, \ldots, r_i]$ be an integer partition of a positive integer $n$ in descending order, where $\forall x \in \left(1, 2, \ldots, i\right), r_x \geq 1$. CRPM (Columnar-Row Partition Moves) is a partizan variant of the CRIM game designed to be played between two players with asymmetric move sets.
  • Player A (Rows): Can only remove entire rows from the partition.
  • Player B (Columns): Can only remove entire columns from the partition.
Like CRIM, after each move the divided parts are merged together. However, unlike CRIM's impartial nature, CRPM creates strategic asymmetry where each player must consider their opponent's limited but potentially powerful move options. The player who cannot make a legal move loses the game.

Moves

CRPM introduces partisan gameplay with player-specific restrictions:

  • Player A (Row Player):
    • Can only select and remove entire rows from the partition
    • Must choose from available rows containing at least one square
    • Cannot perform column operations
  • Player B (Column Player):
    • Can only select and remove entire columns from the partition
    • Must choose from available columns containing at least one square
    • Cannot perform row operations
  • Example Game: Starting with partition $\left(4, 3, 2, 1 \right)$:
    • Move 1 (Player A): Removes row 3, changing partition to $\left(4, 3, 1 \right)$
    • Move 2 (Player B): Removes column 1, changing partition to $\left(3, 2, 1 \right)$
    • Move 3 (Player A): Removes row 2, changing partition to $\left(3, 1 \right)$
    • ...and so forth until one player cannot move

Strategic Implications

The asymmetric nature of CRPM creates several fascinating strategic elements:

  • Complementary Constraints: Players must think about both their own limited options and their opponent's different limitations
  • Zugzwang Potential: Situations where being forced to move puts a player at a disadvantage
  • Endgame Analysis: Different winning/losing positions depending on which player moves first in critical positions
  • Evaluation Complexity: Unlike impartial games, positions must be evaluated separately for each player

Mathematical Properties

As a partizan game, CRPM exhibits properties distinct from its impartial cousin CRIM:

  • Game Value: Positions have values relative to each player, not absolute Grundy numbers
  • First-Player Advantage: The starting player may have inherent advantages in certain partition configurations
  • Strategic Depth: The constraint asymmetry creates rich tactical possibilities despite simplified move rules

More

CRPM represents an exploration into partizan partition games, demonstrating how simple asymmetric constraints can transform the strategic landscape of a familiar game. This variant maintains the elegance of partition manipulation while introducing the complexity and depth characteristic of partizan combinatorial games.
